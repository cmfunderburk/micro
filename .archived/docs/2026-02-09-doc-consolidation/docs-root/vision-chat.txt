  The Implied Priority Stack

  Given your answers, the priority order seems to be:

  1. Matching institutions вҖ” This is the largest gap blocking "all three domains." Bargaining is done. Information/beliefs is partially there. Matching is explicitly acknowledged as ad-hoc.
  2. Information/beliefs becoming causal вҖ” You're actively thinking about this. It's the mechanism that makes information asymmetry studies meaningful.
  3. Equilibrium benchmark вҖ” Needed to interpret results ("how far from efficient?") but doesn't block capability expansion.
  4. Production/sustained economies вҖ” Phase B. Unlocks new research domains but is a larger lift.

  The oracle-vs-process question and negotiation time costs are explicitly deferred pending deeper design work. That's reasonableвҖ”the abstraction is doing useful work right now.

  On the Beliefs Question

  You said you're actively thinking about whether beliefs-for-search-but-not-acceptance is the right choice. Here's how I'd frame the tradeoff:

  Case for current approach (beliefs in search only):
  - Search is about targetingвҖ”you pursue agents you believe are good partners
  - Acceptance is about verificationвҖ”once adjacent, you observe true surplus from the proposed trade
  - This models "optimistic search, verified execution"
  - It's also simpler: acceptance uses ground truth, no need to specify how beliefs affect the bargaining protocol's objective

  Case for beliefs in acceptance:
  - Consistency: if beliefs matter for "should I approach this agent," they should matter for "should I accept this trade"
  - Enables studying how persistent misbeliefs affect outcomes (not just search efficiency)
  - More faithful to incomplete information games where private information isn't revealed at trade time

  The deeper question: What does "acceptance" mean in an incomplete-information world? When Agent A accepts a trade with Agent B:
  - Does A learn B's true type at proposal time? (Then beliefs don't matter for acceptanceвҖ”you've already updated)
  - Or does A decide based on believed type? (Then beliefs should affect acceptance, and trade could be ex-post regretted)

  The answer depends on what the platform is trying to model. If proposals reveal types, the current approach is correct. If proposals don't reveal types, beliefs should affect acceptance.

  On Matching

  The matching gap is interesting because there's a design question embedded in it: what kind of matching institution fits the action-budget model?

  Options:
  1. Centralized clearinghouse (Gale-Shapley style) вҖ” Agents submit preferences, mechanism computes stable matching. Clean theory, but breaks the decentralized action model.
  2. Decentralized search-and-match (Diamond-Mortensen-Pissarides style) вҖ” Random meetings, match surplus thresholds, frictions. Fits the spatial/tick model well. Theory-grounded via search/matching literature.
  3. Proposal markets (current approach, formalized) вҖ” Make the propose/accept/reject mechanism explicit as an institution with known properties. What are the equilibrium properties of "bilateral proposal with cooldowns"?

  Option 3 is interesting because you already have the mechanismвҖ”it just lacks theoretical grounding. Is there literature on decentralized bilateral proposal games that could provide that grounding? Or is the current mechanism genuinely ad-hoc and needs replacement?

  A Question Back

  Given that capability expansion is the priority and matching is the largest gap: do you have a sense of which matching paradigm (centralized, search-theoretic, or formalized-bilateral-proposal) fits the platform's methodology best?
